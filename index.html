Identifying Categories of Tornado-Producing Environments and the Impacts of Climate Change

This project investigates how climate change is shifting the synoptic and mesoscale environments that favor tornado formation. Drawing from 70 years of atmospheric data from ECMWF, NCEI, and NOAA, I applied principal component analysis (PCA) and self-organizing maps (SOMs) to identify, categorize, and track evolving clusters of tornado-favorable environments across the central and eastern U.S.

Using high-resolution reanalysis and GCM outputs from ERA5 and CMIP6, I analyzed multidimensional atmospheric variables such as geopotential height, dewpoint, and wind shear—transforming massive, multi-million-cell datasets in R and MATLAB. This analysis reveals a southeastward shift in tornado-supportive conditions, with new clusters emerging outside traditional Tornado Alley.

Presented at AGU and AAG, this research reflects the power of synoptic climatology to reveal meaningful patterns in an era of intensifying extreme weather.

Tools & Data: R, MATLAB, Self-Organizing Maps, Principal Component Analysis, ERA5, CMIP6, ECMWF, NCEI, NOAA

Standardized Multivariate SOM Classification

This map layout is an output from a Self-Organizing Map (SOM), a type of artificial neural network that clusters similar atmospheric conditions into spatial patterns. Each panel, or node, shows a recurring environment type — built by grouping days with similar atmospheric setups together.

The SOM was trained on multivariable atmospheric data (such as instability indices, temperature gradients, and moisture fields) that had first been simplified using Principal Component Analysis (PCA). These panels do not represent a time sequence — they are not arranged chronologically or directionally (e.g., left-to-right). Instead, each node is a spatial average of cases that belong to that specific pattern type.

For example, if the data input was the Totals Totals Index (TTI), each panel would represent a typical spatial structure of thunderstorm potential. Areas shaded red would suggest higher instability or storm favorability, while blues would indicate lower values. However, this visualization method is flexible and can be applied to a range of variables beyond TTI.

About the Methodology:

To identify subtle and recurring patterns in tornado-supportive environments, I used Self-Organizing Maps (SOMs), which are a form of artificial neural network. SOMs are especially useful for making sense of large, multidimensional atmospheric datasets, as they can cluster similar conditions and visualize them in an intuitive, spatial way. By standardizing the inputs — such as temperature profiles, dewpoint, and wind shear — I was able to train the network to recognize distinct atmospheric patterns that often precede tornado formation.

I also applied Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to simplify the dataset and isolate dominant climate signals, making it easier to interpret how different variables contribute to severe weather potential. Together, SOMs and PCA helped uncover how these environments are shifting over time, both in location and structure, as the climate continues to change.

This approach is inspired by work from Sheridan & Lee (2011), who demonstrated how SOMs can be used effectively in synoptic climatology. Their paper is available here.

Manganese Uptake Research | Hydrogeochemical Lab, Kent State

During my time as a lab technician from 2018–2019 in the Kent State University Hydrogeochemical Lab, I contributed to a research project investigating manganese uptake in plants. This role involved extensive environmental sampling, lab work, and post-processing to support both academic research and environmental quality analysis.

I applied lab-based methods such as X-ray diffraction (XRD), polymerase chain reaction (PCR), and soil grinding techniques using a high-intensity ball mill (metal cylinder with oscillating metal ball) to prepare soil samples for mineralogical and contaminant analysis. Additionally, I collected field samples, prepared them for analysis, and contributed to internal lab reports and summaries of contaminant profiles.

Key equipment used in this study included the X-ray diffraction (XRD) machine (pictured above) to determine mineralogical composition of powdered soil samples — a critical step in assessing trace metal concentrations. The purpose of this machine is to "bounce" x-rays off a surface and record the angle at which the beam diffracts off the crystalline substance. This gives key indications about the interactions and arrangement of the atoms inside the crystal lattice.
