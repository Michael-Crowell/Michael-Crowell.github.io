Identifying Categories of Tornado-Producing Environments and the Impacts of Climate Change

This project investigates how climate change is shifting the synoptic and mesoscale environments that favor tornado formation. Drawing from 70 years of atmospheric data from ECMWF, NCEI, and NOAA, I applied principal component analysis (PCA) and self-organizing maps (SOMs) to identify, categorize, and track evolving clusters of tornado-favorable environments across the central and eastern U.S.

Using high-resolution reanalysis and GCM outputs from ERA5 and CMIP6, I analyzed multidimensional atmospheric variables such as geopotential height, dewpoint, and wind shear—transforming massive, multi-million-cell datasets in R and MATLAB. This analysis reveals a southeastward shift in tornado-supportive conditions, with new clusters emerging outside traditional Tornado Alley.

Presented at AGU and AAG, this research reflects the power of synoptic climatology to reveal meaningful patterns in an era of intensifying extreme weather.

Tools & Data: R, MATLAB, Self-Organizing Maps, Principal Component Analysis, ERA5, CMIP6, ECMWF, NCEI, NOAA

Standardized Multivariate SOM Classification

This map layout is an output from a Self-Organizing Map (SOM), a type of artificial neural network that clusters similar atmospheric conditions into spatial patterns. Each panel, or node, shows a recurring environment type — built by grouping days with similar atmospheric setups together.

The SOM was trained on multivariable atmospheric data (such as instability indices, temperature gradients, and moisture fields) that had first been simplified using Principal Component Analysis (PCA). These panels do not represent a time sequence — they are not arranged chronologically or directionally (e.g., left-to-right). Instead, each node is a spatial average of cases that belong to that specific pattern type.

For example, if the data input was the Totals Totals Index (TTI), each panel would represent a typical spatial structure of thunderstorm potential. Areas shaded red would suggest higher instability or storm favorability, while blues would indicate lower values. However, this visualization method is flexible and can be applied to a range of variables beyond TTI.

About the Methodology:

To identify subtle and recurring patterns in tornado-supportive environments, I used Self-Organizing Maps (SOMs), which are a form of artificial neural network. SOMs are especially useful for making sense of large, multidimensional atmospheric datasets, as they can cluster similar conditions and visualize them in an intuitive, spatial way. By standardizing the inputs — such as temperature profiles, dewpoint, and wind shear — I was able to train the network to recognize distinct atmospheric patterns that often precede tornado formation.

I also applied Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to simplify the dataset and isolate dominant climate signals, making it easier to interpret how different variables contribute to severe weather potential. Together, SOMs and PCA helped uncover how these environments are shifting over time, both in location and structure, as the climate continues to change.

This approach is inspired by work from Sheridan & Lee (2011), who demonstrated how SOMs can be used effectively in synoptic climatology. Their paper is available here.

GIS & Data Science at Portage County Health District

From January 2024 to March 2025, I served as the full-time contracted GIS Technician at the Portage County Health District (PCHD) — though the scope of my work aligned more with that of a Geospatial Data Analyst. As the department's principal data lead, I supported cross-divisional projects that shaped public health strategy, compliance, and outreach. My responsibilities ranged from geocoding and data wrangling to statistical modeling, dashboard development, and map creation. These efforts directly informed decisions by public health managers, environmental health field teams, city councils, and the Health Commissioner herself.

I routinely used ArcGIS Pro, ArcGIS Online, RStudio, Python, and Excel to manage and analyze complex datasets — including bootstrapping, sampling, modeling, and automating weekly updates. I worked with diverse data sources, including the U.S. Census, Ohio EPA, ODNR, local water system data, CDC, and state health records. My contributions included cleaning, transforming, de-identifying, and visualizing datasets into accessible dashboards and maps for use by internal teams and external organizations like the Mental Health & Recovery Board of Kent.

Thematically, my projects spanned:

  • Communicable disease surveillance – maintaining and troubleshooting an interactive COVID-19 dashboard with zip-code level case, hospitalization, and mortality data.
  • Environmental public health – developing a Harmful Algal Bloom (HAB) map and calculating nutrient pollution levels from environmental reports.
  • Food safety analytics – mapping food vendor classifications, inspection violations, and WIC/fresh food access points to inform outreach and enforcement priorities.
  • Regulatory support – assisting with OWTS compliance by geocoding inspection sites and creating visual tools to support Board of Health documentation.
Tools: ArcGIS Pro, ArcGIS Online, R, Python, Excel, Ohio EPA, CDC, U.S. Census

Community Health Data Mapping: StoryMap Highlights

Access to Nutritious Foods — Chronic Disease Connection: This map displays areas within Portage County with varying levels of access to nutritious food retailers such as grocery stores, farmers markets, and WIC-participating vendors. Limited food access is linked to higher rates of chronic illness. I used vendor classification data, county health metrics, and WIC store registries to identify nutritious food deserts. This map helped public health officials locate underserved areas and design outreach programs.

Tools: ArcGIS Online, ArcGIS Pro, Ohio WIC Database, Census Tract Demographics

Drive Time to WIC Retailers: This analysis models drive-time accessibility from residential areas to WIC-participating retailers, highlighting inequities in access to essential nutrition services. It required road network data, spatial buffering, and isochrone modeling. These findings support strategic placement of new vendors and outreach efforts for underserved areas.

Tools: ArcGIS Pro Network Analyst, ODNR Roads Layer, WIC Vendor Data

Census Block Clustering for Vulnerable Populations: Using K-means clustering and census data, this map segments Portage County into statistically defined clusters of vulnerability. Factors include poverty rate, education level, age distribution, and housing stability. The goal was to target public health interventions where they are most needed, based on a holistic demographic risk profile.

Tools: RStudio (K-means), ArcGIS Pro, U.S. Census Data, Python
